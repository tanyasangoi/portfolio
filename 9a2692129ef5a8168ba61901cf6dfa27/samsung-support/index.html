As a UX Intern at Samsung, I worked on a new support initiative called Mail-In Repair. This new service would allow users to mail in their Samsung devices to a service center for repair. I mainly worked on user research for this project. I also worked on designing certain aspects of the entire service.



Problem Background Process Solution Outcome

Problem

The mail-in service was supposed to provide a dual advantage to both the service center and the user. The service center could have better scheduling of repair requests and the user could easily mail the devices in case the service centers are far away. However, the biggest problem with this service was the lack of confidence that the users had in diagnosing the problem with their devices. The current workflow required unnecessary mental effort on the user’s behalf and depended on their self-diagnosis which could have been incorrect. It was extremely time consuming for the user to go through the process of submitting a mail-in request and difficult to correctly understand the instructions to mail-in the device.

Background

The mail-in repair service allowed the user to ship their device to a service centre and receive it back. The user first had to select the type of device that needed to be sent in for repair. Based on the type of device, both mail-in and walk-in repair services were available. The user then had to log in or create a Samsung.com account and register the device which needed to be repaired or chose from the previously registered devices. Following this, the user had to diagnose possible issues with the device. An estimated cost of the repair would be provided to the user and the user would be asked to enter their payment information.


Process

Usability Tests

I began by analyzing 20 usability tests from participants on UserTesting.com. These A/B tested a previously developed version of the prototype. I also conducted talk-aloud usability tests with 5 of my co-workers. The insights I derived from these user tests were then distilled using an affinity map.

Affinity Map

These were some of the insights gathered from the affinity mapping.

Landing Page

  • User does not know where to start on the landing page - A few clicked on the mail- in repair image or support tab ( 3 users)
  • User does not associate “Get Help Now” with starting the mail - in repair (7 users)
  • They would prefer to directed directly to the mail-in process from the landing page ( 2 users)

Registration

  • Most users understand the need for a registration page, however they are unsure of the benefits they receive from the registration
  • They experience a disconnect between the landing page and the registration page
  • They would prefer to directed directly to the mail-in process from the landing page

Issue

  • Users experience a lack of confidence in diagnosing the issue and troubleshooting

Repair Plan

  • Users are unsure of the costs. They question if the costs are associated with the issue diagnosis.
  • Users want to see the estimated time of return

Payment

  • Users do not understand why they are being asked to pay when the service has not been confirmed.

What do users currently like about the page?

  • Ability to opt-in to mailing updates
  • Step format in troubleshooting
  • Not being redirected to a third party website for tracking status
  • Apologetic tone
  • Breadcrumb navigation
  • Information tooltips
  • Not having to talk to customer support

What do the users want?

  • Specific information relating to service center address
  • Detailed information about packaging
  • Ability to print the mailing label
  • More clear status descriptions

What are the general user preferences/habits?

  • They do not like scrolling
  • Users want to be able to do things at their own time and convenience
  • Users do not prefer talking on the phone to customer service
  • Users want more clear status descriptions

Solution

Other than the research I also worked on designing the confirmation and shipping pages in the overall workflow.

The current service request confirmation page was not consistent with the other pages in the workflow. There was no summary of the previous steps included in the confirmation page. The shipping label was hidden and there was no way to save and print the label at a later time. The redesign took these issues into account.

The mailing instructions were extremely wordy and did not clearly explain the device specific shipping requirements. The redesign broke down these instructions into clear and concise steps that were accompanied by a visual for the ease of the user.
