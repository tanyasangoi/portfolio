Contraceptive Vending Machines in India


In rural India, the lack of education, limited access to resources and the taboo surrounding sex are some of the barriers to good sexual health. This project was an attempt to conceptualize a vending machine that could dispense not only sexual health products but also inform the users about safe sex practices. The challenge in designing this vending machine was to be cognizant of the cultural sensitivities while maintaining a good balance between social good and acceptance.


Role Background Problem Process Solution Outcome

Role

For this project, I worked in a team with three of my peers over the duration of three months. I was the primary designer for this project, and also helped with the user research. Given my cultural background, I was able to provide my team members with the context of the traditions and beliefs that helped us refine our user personas.

Problem

In rural India, deep-rooted cultural views about sex and no access to sex education and contraceptive products are coming in the way of reproductive health of the population. Contraceptive vending machines that dispense such products and the knowledge about their use can be used to bridge this gap. In the sensitive cultural climate of India, the risks of vandalism and theft of such machines run high.
How would we design these machines to ensure the privacy of the users and eliminate these risks?

Process

Background

Among developing countries, India has the highest number of women — 31 million — with an "unmet need" for contraception.
The biggest obstacles that prevent women from using contraceptives are lack of knowledge about contraception, health concerns, high costs, limited supplies, and cultural or personal objections. (RAND survey research).

Sex as a subject is considered highly controversial in India. Public discussion of topics of a sexual nature is widely considered as taboo in the Indian society, therefore acting as a barrier to delivery of adequate and effective sexual education to the Indian population.

Comprehensive sexuality education (CSE), which is a curriculum-based sex education program, is recognized globally as a critical component in helping young people assert their sexual reproductive rights. Despite its recognition, CSE is almost nonexistent in India.

Hofstede's Dimensions

According to Hofstede’s cultural dimensions, India is characterized by scoring relatively high in power distance (PDI), low in indulgence, and intermediate individualism.

A high PDI indicates India’s appreciation and acceptance of social hierarchy.

Scoring low on the indulgence index, India has a tendency of cynicism and does not put much emphasis on leisure time and control the gratification of their desires. This may be one of the reasons sex is viewed as taboo.

India is both a collectivistic and individualistic society, scoring an intermediate score in the individualism dimension. The people of Indian are heavily influenced by their group, but also believe people are individually responsible for the way they lead their lives.

Scope

Our team sought to promote SRH through contraceptive vending machines but understood the vending machines could not be a stand-alone product because of the stigma associated with sex and risk of strong opposition. As a result, the contraceptive vending machines will be a complement to SRH programs that already exist. Our team targeted a region of India, Jharkhand, which is predominantly rural and where there are some government programs in place to help educate the community about safe sex and contraceptive usage.

Specifically, the Adolescent Reproductive and Sexual Health (ARSH) project has partnered with governments of Jharkhand to improve young people’s access to ARSH by training health care professionals and implementing community programs. Abuse of the vending machines could be limited or eliminated by restricting the usage of the vending machines to users who have been educated on SRH at specified ARSH supported health centers.

The contraceptive vending machines will be placed not only in ARSH supported health centers but also in places where community-level interventions are held for more accessibility. For instance, in the village of Khunti, families meet periodically facilitated by the Jharkhand government to intended to discuss family planning and improve communication about sexual health. Placing vending machines in the community would allow high accessibility and convenience.

In addition, placing them in safe areas with government support would likely reduce the risks of crime and vandalism compared to placing them in open public spaces. The vending machines will be targeted to married adolescents who have been a part of ARSH supported programs as they are the section of the population that are the most affected by this problem.

Research Plan

Surveys, interviews, and historical analysis were methods we selected to collect data on the general perceptions of users toward the contraceptive vending machines. The surveys and interviews were not completed due to resource limitations and time constraints, but we did conceptualize a plan if the surveys and interviews were conducted. The most important factor in designing the surveys and interviews are making sure the participants feel safe, comfortable, and their privacy is protected due to the negative stigma that exists about the topics. Surveys would be a quick way to elicit answers from a large number of people. In order to achieve this, the team will distribute the surveys at the 154 (as of 2015) adolescent-friendly health centers supported by the ARSH program. The idea is that the participants are already in a safe environment with supportive staff, therefore creating a more comfortable environment. The surveys will be translated into Hindi and other local dialects and will be anonymous. Interviews would allow the team to ask deeper contextual questions. Interviewees will be selected from volunteers at the adolescent-friendly health centers. In order to make the interviewees more comfortable and safe, the team will hire a young female trained interviewer who speaks Hindi and/or the local dialects. In addition, the interviews will be conducted in a private room or other location at or near their home where they would be comfortable talking about sensitive topics. Historical analysis would help the team understand what has been done in the problem space, and specifically what has been effective and what has not. This was completed by the team through online resources and research did in this space in India as a country, but also specifically in Jharkhand. It was also important for the team to understand government policies, regulations, and programs surrounding SRH education.
